## Main Evidence

Thomas 90 closely overlaps with Matthew's invitation to come, take the gentle/easy yoke, and find rest. The Thomas wording is compact and has distinctive phrasing around "my lordship" or "my rule," but the cluster is so close to Matthew that direct or indirect dependence is a major concern.

The logion is not merely a free aphorism; it is an invitation centered on Jesus' yoke and rest. This makes it rhetorically and christologically more developed than many short wisdom sayings in the clean reader.

## Early-Core Assessment

Possible early core:

> Come to me; my yoke is good, and you will find rest.

This is possible, but it cannot yet be separated confidently from the Matthean form.

## Secondary-Layer Indicators

The invitation to come to Jesus, the yoke imagery, and the promise of rest together create a high Matthew-control profile. The Thomas phrase about "lordship" may be an interpretive rendering rather than evidence of a more primitive independent form.

## Limits

No extant Greek Thomas witness is loaded. There is no Mark/Luke double control for this precise unit. Matthew is the dominant control, and one-source control is weaker for reconstructing an earliest sayings layer.
