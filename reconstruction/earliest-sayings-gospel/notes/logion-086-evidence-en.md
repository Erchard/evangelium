## Main Evidence

Thomas 86 preserves the foxes/birds/Son-of-Man homelessness saying in a compact form. Matthew and Luke both preserve a very close form in a discipleship-following context. Thomas lacks that narrative frame and ends with the Son of Man having no place to lay his head and rest.

The saying is aphoristic, memorable, and mobile; it could circulate independently of a narrative setting. Its presence in both Matthew and Luke gives strong external control for the core wording.

## Early-Core Assessment

Possible early core:

> Foxes have holes and birds have nests, but the Son of Man has nowhere to lay his head.

The final rest element in Thomas may preserve an interpretive nuance or a Coptic rendering expansion, but it does not obviously change the core.

## Secondary-Layer Indicators

The main caution is not Thomasine theology but dependence direction. Thomas may preserve a decontextualized form drawn from synoptic-like material. The title "Son of Man" also requires care: it may be early in some Jesus traditions, but it can also be stabilized by later gospel usage.

## Limits

No extant Greek Thomas witness is loaded for this logion. The Thomas form should not be treated as an independent Greek witness. The synoptic controls are strong, but they do not by themselves prove independence from Matthew or Luke.
