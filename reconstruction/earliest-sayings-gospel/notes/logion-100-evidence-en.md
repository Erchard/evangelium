## Main Evidence

Thomas 100 preserves the Caesar/God tribute saying with a distinctive third clause: "and give me what is mine." The Caesar/God core is strongly controlled by Mark, Matthew, and Luke. Thomas's opening is shorter and less explicitly framed as a trap.

The third clause is not present in the synoptic controls and probably reflects Thomasine or collectional interpretation, redirecting the saying toward Jesus' own claim.

## Early-Core Assessment

Possible early core:

> Give Caesar what belongs to Caesar, and give God what belongs to God.

The coin/tax setting is strongly traditional, but the third clause should be separated.

## Secondary-Layer Indicators

"Give me what is mine" may be a secondary christological or Thomasine expansion. The Coptic "gold coin" detail may also reflect adaptation rather than primitive wording.

## Limits

No extant Greek Thomas witness is loaded. Strong triple synoptic control confirms that the core is traditional, but it also raises the possibility that Thomas depends on synoptic-like gospel material.
