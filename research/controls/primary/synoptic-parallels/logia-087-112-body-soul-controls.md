## Control Boundary

Logia 87 and 112 are close enough to control one another internally. Both use body/flesh/soul language and express dependence as a negative condition. This makes them important for understanding Thomas's anthropology, but it does not make them strong evidence for the earliest recoverable sayings gospel.

The methodological rule is:

- short form alone is not enough for clean-reader inclusion;
- internal thematic coherence supports appendix interpretation;
- body/soul or flesh/soul speculation without external control remains outside the clean reconstruction.

## Logion 87 Control

Relevant files:

- `corpus/cards/logion-087.md`
- `reconstruction/earliest-sayings-gospel/notes/logion-087-evidence-en.md`

Logion 87 laments a body dependent on a body and a soul dependent on both. The saying is compact, but its syntax and referent are not transparent. It could be read as a warning against bodily dependence, a critique of layered embodiment, or a theological reflection on the soul's captivity.

Main interpretive options:

1. Ascetic anthropology: the body becomes wretched when it depends only on another body.
2. Soul-captivity reading: the soul is endangered when it depends on body and embodied relations.
3. Thomasine reflective layer: the saying belongs to later internal teaching about body, soul, and dependence.
4. Early aphoristic fragment: possible in form, but not recoverable with enough external control.

Control decision:

- Logion 87 has no loaded extant Greek Thomas witness.
- It has no direct canonical wording control.
- Logion 112 is the closest internal control; Logion 29 supplies a broader flesh/spirit/body neighbor.
- The evidence supports appendix explanation, not clean-reader inclusion.

Reconstruction implication: keep Logion 87 outside the clean reader. If retroverted into Greek later, label it as `Greek retroversion, hypothetical`.

## Logion 112 Control

Relevant files:

- `corpus/cards/logion-112.md`
- `reconstruction/earliest-sayings-gospel/notes/logion-112-evidence-en.md`

Logion 112 laments flesh dependent on soul and soul dependent on flesh. It is even more symmetrical than Logion 87 and reads like a compact anthropological maxim.

Main interpretive options:

1. Mutual-dependence warning: flesh and soul both become wretched when each is defined by the other.
2. Anti-dualistic correction: the saying may warn against making either flesh or soul ultimate.
3. Dualistic risk: the language can also be read as late body/soul reflection.
4. Appendix teaching unit: important for Thomas's profile, but not securely early.

Control decision:

- Logion 112 has no loaded extant Greek Thomas witness.
- It has no direct synoptic or canonical wording control.
- Logion 87 is the closest internal control, and Logion 29 is a broader anthropology neighbor.
- The saying remains too reflective and undercontrolled for clean-reader inclusion.

Reconstruction implication: keep Logion 112 outside the clean reader. Use it in the appendix to explain the body/soul strand.
