## Reconstructed Early Core

English:

> Nothing is hidden that will not become manifest.

Ukrainian:

> Немає нічого прихованого, що не стане явним.

## Marked Expansion

English:

> Know what is before your face, and what is hidden from you will be revealed to you.

Ukrainian:

> Пізнай те, що перед твоїм обличчям, і те, що приховане від тебе, відкриється тобі.

## Coptic Witness

Source: `sources/primary_texts/coptic/logia-001-007-coptic-working.md`.

The Coptic witness preserves the whole saying as a command to know what is before one's face followed by the hidden/revealed maxim.

## Greek Witness

Source: `sources/primary_texts/greek_poxy/poxy654-transcription-working.md` and `sources/primary_texts/greek_poxy/dclp-poxy654-62840.xml`.

P.Oxy. 654, lines 27-31, preserves a Greek version of the saying with lacunae. Line 28 includes the variant `κεκαλυμμένον` / `κεκρυμμένον`.

## Synoptic Parallels

The hidden/revealed maxim has close thematic parallels in Mark 4:22, Matthew 10:26, Luke 8:17, and Luke 12:2. These parallels support the possibility of an early shared saying, though the Thomasine "before your face" frame requires separate analysis.

Detailed synoptic table: `controls/synoptic-parallels/logion-005-hidden-revealed.md`.

The new comparison strengthens the early-core assessment. Luke 8:17 is especially close to the hidden/manifest wording, while Matthew 10:26 and Luke 12:2 are close to the covered/revealed form. Mark 4:22 preserves the same motif in a different, purpose-oriented formulation. None of these parallels explains the Thomasine "before your face" command.

## Rationale

Reasons for inclusion:

- The hidden/revealed maxim is short and multiply attested in early gospel traditions.
- The Greek witness confirms pre-Coptic circulation.
- The saying can stand independently from the Thomasine command that precedes it.

Reasons for marking the expansion:

- The "before your face" command is characteristic of a wisdom/self-knowledge frame.
- The saying's order in Thomas may reflect redactional shaping.
- The Greek line remains partly reconstructed.
