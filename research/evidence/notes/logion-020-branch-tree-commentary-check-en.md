# Logion 20 Branch/Tree Commentary Check

Status: targeted commentary/source check v0.1.

## Question

Does the branch/tree variation in the mustard-seed parable strengthen the case for moving Thomas 20 from `UNCERTAIN` to `INCLUDE_WITH_MARKER`?

Short answer: no, not yet.

The variation strengthens the case that Thomas 20 is important and may preserve a mobile parable form, but it does not resolve the direction of dependence or the textual layer problem created by the lack of a Greek Thomas 20 witness.

## What The Branch/Tree Variation Shows

Thomas 20 has a "great branch" / shelter image. Early Christian Writings lists several control translations that render the same Coptic feature as "great branch," "enormous foliage," or "big stalk." This confirms that the Coptic wording is not naturally the same as Matthew/Luke's "tree."

Mark 4:32 has the closest canonical parallel at this point because it speaks of "large branches" rather than a tree. Matthew 13:32 and Luke 13:19 both move to "tree" language.

Therefore:

- Thomas is closer to Mark than to Matthew/Luke in the branch motif.
- Thomas is not simply identical to Mark, because Thomas has a singular large branch and "shelter" rather than Mark's plural branches and shadow.
- Matthew/Luke "tree" could be a normal retelling, an allusive intensification, or a redactional move toward a more explicit great-tree image.

## Is Matthew/Luke "Tree" Secondary?

It is plausible but not proven.

The open overview and scholarly summaries note that the parable is often read through a small-beginning / large-growth contrast, and that the birds may evoke great-tree imagery such as Daniel 4 or Ezekiel 17. That makes Matthew/Luke's "tree" language understandable as an intensification of the image.

However, the same tradition could naturally vary between plant, branch, shrub, and tree language without a single linear development. The evidence does not allow a clean sequence:

1. Thomas/Mark branch form;
2. later Matthew/Luke tree form.

That sequence is possible, but not demonstrated.

## Does Thomas's Singular "Large Branch" Look Primitive?

Not decisively.

It could be:

- a more restrained form than Matthew/Luke's "tree";
- an abbreviated form related to Mark's "large branches";
- a Coptic-preserved variant;
- a Thomasine reshaping that emphasizes shelter rather than botanical scale.

Linssen's close Coptic reading is useful because it stresses the exact Coptic sequence: the seed falls on worked earth, puts forth a great branch, and becomes shelter. His interpretive model is highly Thomas-specific and should not be adopted uncritically, but his attention to the Coptic wording supports keeping "worked earth" and "great branch" visible in our apparatus.

## Does This Justify `INCLUDE_WITH_MARKER`?

Not yet.

The early core remains strong:

> The kingdom is like a mustard seed: it is small, but it grows and gives shelter to the birds of heaven.

But the current commentary check does not remove the main limits:

- no loaded Greek witness for Thomas 20;
- no proof that Thomas is independent of Mark or synoptic-like tradition;
- no full academic collation with major Thomas commentaries;
- branch/tree variation is suggestive, not decisive.

## Decision

Later project decision: Logion 20 has been moved to `INCLUDE_WITH_MARKER` for the short mustard-seed core.

Confidence for the included core is medium.

The branch/tree and dependence questions remain marked, but the short core is now in the Ukrainian reader text.
