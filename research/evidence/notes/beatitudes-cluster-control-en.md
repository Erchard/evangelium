# Beatitudes Cluster-Control Note v0.1

Status: second cluster-control pass, 2026-07-18.

## Scope

This note controls the beatitude group around Logia 54, 58, 68, and 69.

Primary logia:

- 54: blessed are the poor, for the kingdom belongs to you / is yours.
- 58: blessed is the person who labored and found life.
- 68: blessed when hated and persecuted.
- 69: blessed are those persecuted in the heart; blessed are the hungry, for they will be satisfied.

Related logia: 7, 79, and 103 contain beatitude or beatitude-like forms, but they are not primary targets of this pass.

## Cluster Diagnosis

The beatitude form is a genuine early-tradition form, but not every Thomas beatitude carries the same reconstructive weight.

The cluster contains at least three different kinds of material:

1. A short poor/kingdom beatitude with strong external control: Logion 54.
2. Persecution and hunger beatitudes with strong canonical motif control but distinctive Thomasine shaping: Logia 68 and 69.
3. A wisdom/labor/life beatitude with plausible early style but weak direct control: Logion 58.

The cluster therefore supports the current selective clean reader model. It strengthens Logion 54, but it does not require adding the other beatitudes to the clean reconstruction.

## External Control

Logion 54 has the clearest control. Luke 6:20 preserves a very close direct-poverty and second-person kingdom form. Matthew 5:3 preserves the related "poor in spirit" form and the kingdom-of-heaven wording. Thomas 54 stands between these forms: it is short like an early aphorism, closer to Luke on "poor" and second person, but closer to Matthew in "kingdom of heaven."

Logia 68 and 69 have real canonical controls, especially Matthew 5:6, Matthew 5:10-12, Luke 6:21-23, and Luke 6:22-23. The problem is not lack of contact. The problem is that Thomas reshapes the material: persecution becomes interiorized, "knowing the Father" enters the logic, and Logion 68 has a difficult ending about finding no place where persecution happened.

Logion 58 has no close synoptic control in the current project. James 1:12 and broader wisdom/endurance traditions may help interpretation, but they do not provide enough control for clean-reader inclusion.

## Reader-Layer Implication

No clean-reader expansion is recommended by this pass.

Logion 54 remains included with marker because it is compact, strongly controlled, and plausible as an early beatitude core. Logia 58, 68, and 69 remain outside the clean reader because their current forms are either weakly controlled, composite, or distinctively reshaped.

## Appendix-Layer Implication

The appendix should treat the beatitude cluster as a major reader-facing explanation of method:

- Similar form alone is not enough for inclusion.
- Strong canonical parallels do not automatically prove that the Thomas form is early.
- Logion 54 is included because the short form is controlled and stable.
- Logia 68 and 69 are important because they show Thomas reshaping beatitude material around persecution, interiority, knowledge, hunger, and desire.
- Logion 58 should be presented as plausible wisdom material whose current evidence is not yet strong enough.

## Evidence Dossier Use

In the English evidence dossier, this cluster should support a methodological section on selective inclusion within a shared form-type:

- The project does not include a saying merely because it sounds early.
- It does not exclude related material merely because it is non-canonical.
- It separates external control, internal Thomasine coherence, and publication confidence.
