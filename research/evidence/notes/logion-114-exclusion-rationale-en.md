# Logion 114 Publication-Level Exclusion Rationale

Status: publication-facing rationale v0.1, 2026-07-18.

## Decision

Logion 114 is excluded from the clean reconstructed earliest sayings gospel.

Decision label: `EXCLUDE_AS_SECONDARY`.

This exclusion is not a dismissal of the text and not an attempt to hide a difficult ending. Logion 114 remains in the full appendix with Coptic text, translation, interpretation, and source/control references. The decision means only that this unit does not fit the project's criteria for the earliest recoverable sayings-gospel layer.

## Textual Witness Status

The preserved witness for Logion 114 in this project is the Coptic NHC II,2 text.

No loaded Oxyrhynchus Greek Thomas witness preserves this logion. P.Oxy. 1, P.Oxy. 654, and P.Oxy. 655 do not provide direct support for it in the loaded corpus.

Because the logion is excluded as secondary, a Greek retroversion is not a priority. If a Greek retroversion is ever supplied for comparison, it must be labeled `Greek retroversion, hypothetical` and must not be treated as a manuscript witness.

## What The Logion Says

The unit is a dialogue scene. Simon Peter says that Mary should leave because women are not worthy of life. Jesus responds that he will lead her and make her male so that she may become a living spirit like the male disciples. The final sentence generalizes the condition: every woman who makes herself male will enter the kingdom of heaven.

This is not a compact aphorism, parable, or short wisdom saying. It is a community-boundary scene involving named figures, gendered worthiness, transformation language, and access to the kingdom.

## Why It Looks Secondary

Several features point away from the earliest recoverable sayings-gospel layer.

First, the form is scenic and dialogical. Many clean-reader units in this project are short sayings, aphoristic cores, or parable cores with external controls. Logion 114 instead stages a dispute between Peter, Mary, and Jesus.

Second, the topic is developed community identity. The logion is concerned with whether Mary and women belong among the disciples and with the symbolic conditions under which they may enter life or the kingdom. This looks like a later community debate about authority and inclusion, not a compact early Jesus saying.

Third, the gender-transformation language is highly marked. The idea that a woman must become male to enter the kingdom belongs to a developed symbolic anthropology. It may be a spiritual metaphor in its own ancient setting, but it is not safely recoverable as an earliest sayings-gospel core.

Fourth, internal Thomas parallels do not solve the problem. Logion 22 uses male/female unity language, but it does not contain Peter's exclusion of Mary or the claim that women are unworthy of life. Logion 13 offers broad disciple-authority context, but it does not directly control the wording or form of Logion 114.

Fifth, there is no direct synoptic wording control and no loaded Greek Thomas witness. The Coptic text is important, but the project has no independent early witness that would justify moving this ending into the clean reconstruction.

## Responsible Reader Explanation

A modern reader may understandably ask whether the exclusion is ideological. The answer should be clear: the project excludes Logion 114 for form-critical, textual, and redactional reasons, not because the text is embarrassing.

The edition should still show the logion. It should let the reader inspect the Coptic witness, the translation, the internal Thomas parallels, and the rationale. Transparency is part of the method: excluded logia remain available for study, especially when they are historically important.

Logion 114 is important for the history of Thomas, Mary traditions, Peter traditions, gendered spiritual language, and community-boundary debates. It is not, under the present evidence, part of the reconstructed earliest sayings gospel.

## Publication Use

In the evidence dossier, Logion 114 should serve as the clearest example of secondary exclusion:

- the text is fully preserved in the appendix;
- the reason for exclusion is explicit;
- no clean-reader membership changes are made;
- the argument is historical-critical rather than polemical;
- the reader can distinguish historical importance from reconstruction inclusion.

## Final Implication

Keep Logion 114 out of the clean reconstructed text.

Keep Logion 114 in the full appendix and evidence dossier as a transparent, fully explained exclusion case.
