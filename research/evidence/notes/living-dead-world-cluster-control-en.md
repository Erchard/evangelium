# Living / Dead / World Cluster-Control Note v0.1

Status: first cluster-control pass, 2026-07-17.

## Scope

This note controls the living/dead/world group that repeatedly appears in the Gospel of Thomas and currently keeps several logia outside the clean reconstruction.

Primary logia:

- 11: heavens passing, living/dead, eating the dead, one/two.
- 18: beginning/end and not tasting death.
- 52: the living one before you versus the dead prophets.
- 56: knowing the world and finding a corpse.
- 59: seeing the Living One while alive.
- 60: Samaritan, lamb, corpse, and rest.
- 80: knowing the world and finding the body.
- 111: heaven/earth passing, living from the Living One, not seeing death, world unworthy.

Related logia: 1 and 19 for deathlessness; 3, 67, and 70 for self-knowledge; 110 for world-renunciation.

## Cluster Diagnosis

The cluster is real and important, but it is not one stable early saying. It combines several overlapping motifs:

1. Deathlessness or not tasting death.
2. Recognition of the Living One while one is still alive.
3. The world as corpse/body or as something unworthy.
4. Living/dead reversal imagery, including eating, corpse, and rest language.
5. A heaven/earth passing formula attached to the larger Thomasine complex.

These motifs are mutually intelligible inside Thomas, but they do not all have the same evidential weight. The project should therefore avoid treating the whole cluster as one recoverable earliest-gospel unit.

## External Control

The strongest external control belongs to the heaven/earth passing formula in Logia 11 and 111. This has clear synoptic parallels in Mark 13:31, Matthew 24:35, and Luke 21:33. The controlled unit is the heaven/earth passing saying, not the full Thomasine complexes around it.

The deathlessness formula has a partial control channel through the "not taste death" idiom, especially Mark 9:1, Matthew 16:28, and Luke 9:27. In Thomas, however, the phrase is moved into a realized-knowledge and self-recognition frame. That means it can support an appendix discussion and possible Greek retroversion of a subunit, but it does not by itself justify clean-reader inclusion of Logia 18 or 111.

The world/corpse/body material in Logia 56 and 80 is internally strong but externally weak. It is conceptually central to Thomas's worldview, but it currently lacks enough independent external control to stand as part of the earliest recoverable reader.

Logia 52, 59, and 60 are best treated as internal Thomasine expansions or interpretive scenes around the living/dead contrast. They are valuable for reader commentary, but not suitable for the clean reconstruction.

## Reader-Layer Implication

No clean-reader expansion is recommended by this pass.

The reader should remain focused on sayings with stronger external control, papyrus witness, or defensible canonical-control reconstruction. This cluster belongs primarily in the second layer: the full appendix and English evidence dossier.

## Appendix-Layer Implication

This cluster should become one of the main explanatory guideposts for readers. The appendix sections for Logia 11, 18, 52, 56, 59, 60, 80, and 111 should explain:

- why these sayings are fascinating and probably meaningful inside Thomas;
- why internal thematic coherence is not the same as earliest-layer recoverability;
- which subunits have external control;
- why the project prints them in the appendix rather than the clean reconstruction.

Logion 80 is the practical gain of this pass: it no longer stands as an unsupported deferred logion. It remains without a direct evidence note, but it now has a cluster-control rationale.

## Evidence Dossier Use

In the English evidence dossier, this cluster should support a methodological section on negative control:

- The project does not exclude sayings because they are uninteresting.
- It excludes or defers sayings when the evidence cannot separate early recoverable tradition from later Thomasine theological arrangement.
- The clean reconstruction is therefore deliberately narrower than the full Gospel of Thomas.
