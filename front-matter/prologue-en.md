## Prologue

The Gospels were not written down immediately after the earthly life of Christ. Several decades passed between the events themselves and the texts that preserved them.

For the ancient world, this was not unusual. Still, every attentive reader of the Gospels knows that Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John do not always tell the story in exactly the same way. That is why there are four witnesses. Each preserves its own testimony.

How precious it would be to have an even earlier witness. Something closer to the time when the words of Jesus were still being passed from disciple to disciple.

For a long time, it seemed that no such text had survived.

Then a remarkable text was found, now conventionally known as the Gospel of Thomas. It did not enter the canon, but it is sharply different from most apocryphal writings. It is not filled with fantastic stories about the childhood of Jesus, elaborate myths about heavenly worlds, or later legendary embellishments.

In form, it is almost entirely a collection of short sayings of Jesus.

That is what makes it so important.

Before a great narrative appears, there is often a memory of words. The disciples may not have written a full story at once, but they could have preserved very early what the Teacher said.

Over time, it became clear that this text is not all of one age. It contains later additions. Yet within it one can discern an earlier core: shorter, simpler sayings that stand closer to the first memory of Jesus.

What follows is an attempt to recover that early core.

This text is called the Logia of Jesus, that is, the Words of Jesus. It is not a biography of Christ, nor a replacement for the Gospels. It is an attempt to listen carefully for the earliest sound of his teaching, as far as it can still be heard through the sources that remain.

For the one who seeks God, this is a rare opportunity: to read not a later account of faith, but brief words that may stand near the very beginning of Christian proclamation.
